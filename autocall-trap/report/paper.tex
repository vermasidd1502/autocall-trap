% ABSTRACT
\newpage\begin{center}{\Large\bfseries Abstract}\end{center}\vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent Retail autocallable notes are among the fastest-growing structured products, yet their pricing remains opaque. This paper prices 20 real autocallable notes sourced from SEC EDGAR under both GBM and the Heston stochastic volatility model (QE scheme, 50,000 Monte Carlo paths, per-underlying calibration, discrete dividends, backward-adjusted prices). The Heston model reveals an average hidden margin of 3.02\% versus the SEC-admitted 3.22\% --- with high cross-sectional dispersion from $-3.3\%$ (ORCL, fairly priced) to $+16.3\%$ (VLO, massively overpriced). A quintile sort on the Structural Complexity Premium factor shows Q5 (most overpriced) notes carry an average SCP of 8.43\%, representing the capturable margin for institutional desks via synthetic replication with exchange-traded options. Regime analysis confirms the mispricing is procyclical: the ES gap widens substantially for high-vol issuances versus low-vol issuances. Of the 20 notes, 16 autocalled (avg return +3.4\%), 3 matured at par (+19.1\% avg), and one --- ROKU --- suffered a catastrophic knock-in loss of $-72.2\%$. Historical prices are backward-adjusted for corporate actions, correcting a prior false disaster classification of the NVDA note (which correctly autocalled at $+12.3\%$ after accounting for the June 2024 10-for-1 stock split). Fama-MacBeth cross-sectional regressions with Newey-West standard errors are applied to validate the SCP factor, though the 20-note sample lacks sufficient statistical power for definitive inference.

\newpage\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
Autocallable structured notes exceed \$100 billion in annual US issuance \citep{vokata2021}. They offer above-market coupons in exchange for bearing complex, path-dependent downside risk. The buyer pays par (\$1,000); the question is whether that price is fair.

The hypothesis: \textbf{retail autocallable notes are systematically mispriced under flat-volatility models, and that mispricing constitutes an exploitable alternative factor.} The issuer prices using stochastic vol models with full access to the implied volatility surface. The retail buyer uses GBM at best. This information asymmetry creates a Structural Complexity Premium (SCP) that persists because the buyer lacks the tools to detect it \citep{celerier2017}.

This paper makes five contributions: (1) a complete pricing framework under both GBM and Heston with QE simulation, per-underlying calibration, and discrete dividends; (2) Expected Shortfall analysis quantifying disaster severity, not just probability; (3) sector and industry analysis of where the SCP concentrates; (4) an empirical backtest across 20 real EDGAR-sourced notes with backward-adjusted prices, corrected outcome reconstruction, and Fama-MacBeth regression validation; (5) actionable investment strategies: long-only SCP screening, synthetic short replication via options, regime-conditional allocation, and ESG-integrated filtering.

% 2. LITERATURE REVIEW
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\newpage\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:lit}
\citet{henderson2011} found structured equity products overpriced by 8\% on average. \citet{wallmeier2009} documented 3--5\% margins in Swiss capital-protected products, with complexity correlating to margin. \citet{deng2011} modelled autocallable pricing and found substantial issuer profits. \citet{vokata2021} analysed 28,000+ US products, finding $\sim$7\% average embedded fees. \citet{celiere2023} estimated 3--7\% margins across 2,000+ US autocallables.

On methodology: \citet{heston1993} introduced stochastic volatility with a closed-form characteristic function. \citet{andersen2008} developed the QE scheme eliminating negative-variance bias. \citet{albrecher2007} corrected the ``little Heston trap'' instability. \citet{acerbi2002} established Expected Shortfall as a coherent risk measure, adopted under Basel III/IV \citep{bcbs2019}. \citet{celerier2017} coined ``catering through complexity,'' showing issuers raise complexity to extract margins.

\textbf{Note on terminology:} In institutional private debt, securitised credit, and CLO markets, the term ``structural complexity premium'' is widely used to describe the \emph{excess yield paid to investors} as compensation for underwriting bespoke, illiquid, and structurally opaque instruments \citep{celerier2017}. In that context, complexity benefits the informed buyer. This paper adapts and inverts the concept for the retail structured equity market: here, the Structural Complexity Premium (SCP) denotes the \emph{hidden margin extracted from the investor} by the issuing bank, enabled by the same opacity that, in institutional markets, would command a yield premium. The directional reversal---from investor compensation to issuer extraction---is the central insight, and reflects the information asymmetry inherent in the retail distribution channel.

\textbf{Gaps addressed:} (1) no prior ES comparison between GBM and Heston for autocallables; (2) procyclicality not empirically demonstrated; (3) mispricing not framed as a tradeable factor.

% 3. PRODUCT STRUCTURE
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\newpage\section{Product Structure}\label{sec:product}
The investor bets that the underlying stock will not crash below the knock-in barrier (typically 50--70\% of initial price) over 1--5 years. In exchange, they collect quarterly coupons of 6--17\% annualised. If the stock performs well, the note autocalls early and returns par plus coupons. If the barrier is breached, the investor bears the full downside.

\begin{table}[H]\centering\small
\caption{Representative Term Sheet (ORCL Note, JPMorgan, Jan 2021)}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}\toprule
\textbf{Parameter}&\textbf{Value}\\\midrule
Underlying&ORCL at \$54.92\\Par&\$1,000\\Maturity&2 years (8 quarterly obs)\\
Coupon&2.125\%/quarter (8.5\% p.a.)\\Autocall Trigger&100\% of initial\\
Coupon Barrier&70\%\\Knock-In Barrier&60\%\\Memory Coupon&Yes\\
Estimated Value&\$965.70 (3.4\% admitted margin)\\\bottomrule
\end{tabular}\end{table}

The payoff is path-dependent: at each observation, three checks run sequentially. \textit{Coupon}: paid if stock $\geq$ coupon barrier; deferred (not lost) under memory feature if below. \textit{Autocall}: if stock $\geq$ initial price, note terminates, par returned. \textit{Maturity}: if barrier never breached, par returned; if breached, investor receives $\$1{,}000 \times (S_T/S_0)$, bearing full loss.

\newpage\section{Sector and Industry Analysis}\label{sec:sectors}
Our 20-note sample spans 18 underlyings across technology (ORCL, NVDA, AMD, ROKU, MSFT, PYPL), consumer (DIS, MGM, GM, F), energy (VLO, ICLN), financials (BAC, BA), and broad indices (SPX, SPY). Using actual realised volatility at issuance as a proxy for ATM implied vol, the dispersion is substantial: VLO carried 78.1\% IV at issuance (Jan 2021, post-COVID energy vol), NVDA carried 67.5\% (Apr 2022, tech selloff), ORCL carried 56.5\% (Feb 2026), while low-vol names like ORCL (Jan 2021, 18.2\%) and SPX (23.4\%) anchor the bottom. The SCP varies accordingly: high-vol single stocks carry SCP of 5--16\%, while low-vol indices carry 0.7--1.4\%. Goldman Sachs notes average the highest admitted margins (5--10\%), consistent with \citet{vokata2021}'s cross-issuer findings.

% 5. PRICING METHODOLOGY
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\newpage\section{Pricing Methodology}\label{sec:method}
\subsection{GBM Benchmark}
$dS = (r-q)S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$, with $\sigma$ set to the ATM realised vol at each note's trade date. Discrete dividends applied at ex-dates.

\subsection{Heston Stochastic Volatility with Per-Underlying Calibration}
\vspace{-4pt}\begin{align}
dS &= (r-q)S\,dt + \sqrt{v}\,S\,dW_S,\quad dv = \kappa(\theta-v)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v}\,dW_v,\quad \text{corr}(dW_S,dW_v)=\rho
\end{align}

For each of the 18 underlyings, we calibrate $(\kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho, v_0)$ from the realised ATM volatility at the trade date and the market regime:
\begin{table}[H]\centering\small
\caption{Regime-Dependent Heston Calibration}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}\toprule
\textbf{Regime}&$\kappa$&$\rho$&$\xi$ scaling\\\midrule
2021 (Low-Vol)&2.5&$-0.55$&$1.8\times\sigma$\\
2022 (High-Vol)&1.8&$-0.72$&$1.8\times\sigma$\\
2023+ (Recovery)&2.0&$-0.65$&$1.8\times\sigma$\\\bottomrule
\end{tabular}\end{table}

$v_0 = \sigma^2$, $\theta = 1.05\,v_0$. Feller condition verified for all 20 notes. The QE scheme \citep{andersen2008} with 15 substeps avoids negative-variance flooring bias. 100,000 Monte Carlo paths per note.

\subsection{Expected Shortfall}
$\text{ES}_\alpha = \mathbb{E}[X \mid X \leq \text{VaR}_\alpha]$. Computed at both 5\% and 1\% levels. Measures average disaster severity, not just threshold \citep{acerbi2002}.

\newpage\section{Factor Description and Investment Strategies}\label{sec:factor}
\subsection{The SCP Factor}
$\text{SCP}_i = (1000 - \text{FV}_{\text{Heston},i})/1000$. Constructed from SEC EDGAR filings (term sheets), implied vol surfaces (OptionMetrics/Bloomberg), and Yahoo Finance prices. Factor properties: procyclical magnitude, cross-sectional variation (single-stock $>$ index), complexity dependence, issuer variation, persistence.

\subsection{Strategy 1: Long-Only vs Benchmark} Buy only Q1 notes (lowest SCP, fairest pricing) and compare risk-adjusted returns to a passive S\&P~500 allocation over the same holding period. This replaces the prior long-short framing: retail autocallable notes are bespoke, illiquid OTC contracts that cannot be borrowed or sold short in the secondary market. The practical question is whether an SCP-screened portfolio of fairly-priced notes delivers alpha over a passive benchmark.

\subsection{Strategy 1b: Synthetic Short Replication} For quantitative hedge funds seeking to exploit Q5 (most overpriced) notes, the short leg can be implemented synthetically. A desk replicates the note's economic exposure using exchange-traded options: (i) sell a down-and-in put spread mimicking the knock-in barrier, (ii) buy digital calls replicating the contingent coupon stream, (iii) delta-hedge the residual. The spread between the issuer's retail offer price and the cost of the institutional replication portfolio captures the SCP margin directly.

\subsection{Strategy 2: Regime-Conditional} Low VIX ($<$18): allocate 10--15\% to autocallables. High VIX ($>$25): eliminate exposure.

\subsection{Strategy 3: ESG-Integrated} Three-level screen: (1) underlying ESG $\geq$ A (MSCI), reducing tail risk; (2) issuer governance top quartile, reducing margin extraction; (3) Sustainalytics controversy filter, removing event-driven gap risk. Combined filter: SCP $<$ median, ESG $\geq$ A, VIX $<$ 22, KI $\leq$ 60\%.

\newpage\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
The corrected backtest produces a more nuanced picture than the original. The average SCP of 3.02\% is close to the SEC-admitted average of 3.22\%, but this average masks extreme cross-sectional dispersion: SCP ranges from $-3.3\%$ (ORCL, fairly or favourably priced) to $+16.3\%$ (VLO, massively overpriced). The concentration of high SCP in volatile single stocks with aggressive barrier levels is consistent with the ``catering through complexity'' hypothesis \citep{celerier2017}.

\textbf{The NVDA correction is consequential.} The original paper reported NVDA as a $-77.6\%$ disaster note, a flagship example of SCP-predicted failure. This was entirely caused by an unadjusted stock split in the historical price data. With backward-adjusted prices, NVDA autocalled at $+12.3\%$. This correction eliminates one of the paper's strongest individual case studies but strengthens the methodology: a result that survives data cleaning is more credible than one that does not.

\textbf{The ROKU note is instructive.} It carried a near-zero SCP ($+0.01\%$), meaning the Heston model assessed it as fairly priced. The investor accepted a correctly-priced bet on an extremely volatile stock, and the tail event materialised. This is not mispricing --- it is risk. The SCP factor's value lies in identifying notes where the \emph{price is wrong}, not where the \emph{risk is high}.

\textit{Methodological corrections (v2):} (1) Historical prices are backward-adjusted for stock splits and corporate actions via Yahoo Finance's adjusted close, with the initial price $S_0$ re-based to the adjusted series. (2) Outcome reconstruction correctly allows stock recovery after knock-in barrier breach --- a note that breaches the barrier but recovers above $S_0$ at maturity now correctly classifies as ``matured at par.'' (3) The impractical long-short strategy has been replaced with long-only vs benchmark and synthetic short replication frameworks. (4) Fama-MacBeth cross-sectional regressions with Newey-West standard errors correct for serial correlation from overlapping return horizons.

\textit{Limitations:} Heston calibrated from 60-day trailing realised vol (not option-implied surfaces); 20-note sample produces 4 notes per quintile, insufficient for statistical significance; issuer credit risk not modelled; risk-free rates pulled from FRED 2-year Treasury may not match the note's exact funding rate. \textit{Extensions:} Scale to 200+ notes via the EDGAR EFTS bulk pipeline; integrate WRDS OptionMetrics for true implied vol surfaces; expand Fama-MacBeth to a multi-factor model controlling for sector, maturity, VIX regime, and issuer fixed effects.

\section{Conclusion}
The corrected analysis produces four key findings: (1) The average Heston-computed SCP is \textbf{3.02\%} with a range of $-3.3\%$ to $+16.3\%$, revealing that some notes are fairly priced while others embed massive hidden margins. (2) The SCP concentrates in high-vol single stocks with aggressive barrier levels, exactly where retail investors are most disadvantaged. (3) Q5 notes carry an average capturable margin of \textbf{8.43\%}, exploitable by institutional desks via synthetic options replication. (4) The sole genuine disaster in the sample (ROKU, $-72.2\%$) carried a near-zero SCP, confirming that the Heston model correctly distinguished between mispriced notes and correctly-priced risky bets. Statistical validation requires scaling to $N \geq 200$ notes. The infrastructure is built. The trap is real.

\section{Glossary}
\begin{description}[leftmargin=2cm,style=nextline,nosep]
\item[Structured Note] Debt security with return linked to an underlying asset.
\item[Autocallable] Terminates early if underlying exceeds trigger on observation dates.
\item[Knock-In] Price threshold activating a hidden put, exposing investor to downside.
\item[Memory Coupon] Accumulates missed coupons; pays all when barrier is cleared.
\item[Stochastic Volatility] Model where vol itself is random, producing fat tails.
\item[Expected Shortfall] Average loss given you are in the worst $\alpha\%$ of outcomes.
\item[SCP] Structural Complexity Premium: the hidden margin in a note's pricing.
\end{description}
