\begin{center}
{\Large\bfseries Abstract}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.5cm}

\noindent
Retail autocallable contingent income barrier notes are among the fastest-growing structured products, yet their pricing remains opaque to individual investors. This paper develops a quantitative framework for detecting embedded issuer margin by pricing 20 real autocallable notes sourced from SEC EDGAR under two competing stochastic models: Geometric Brownian Motion (the naive flat-vol benchmark) and the Heston stochastic volatility model, simulated via the Quadratic Exponential scheme with per-underlying calibration and discrete dividends.

Across 20 notes spanning 5 issuers and 18 underlyings, the Heston model reveals an average hidden margin of 4.80\% versus the SEC-admitted 3.22\%, an additional 1.58\% the issuer captures beyond what they disclose. The true severity emerges in the tails: the Expected Shortfall at the 5\% level diverges by \$75 on average between models; at the 1\% level, by \$111. A quintile sort on the Structural Complexity Premium factor produces a Q1--Q5 long-short spread of +17.6 percentage points in realised returns. Regime analysis confirms the mispricing is procyclical: the ES gap averages \$90 for 2022 high-vol issuances versus \$47 for 2021 low-vol issuances. Three notes that suffered knock-in losses (ROKU $-67\%$, NVDA $-78\%$, ICLN $-12\%$) all carried above-median SCP scores.

This hidden margin is framed as a \textbf{Structural Complexity Premium}, an alternative factor exploitable through quantamental strategies combining SEC filing analysis, volatility surface data, and ESG-adjusted issuer screening. Investment strategies utilising this factor, including a long-short approach and ESG-integrated portfolio construction, are proposed. These findings have implications for retail investor protection, FINRA suitability frameworks, and the broader debate on transparency in structured product markets.

\section{Introduction: Research Proposal}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{The Research Question}

Autocallable structured notes have experienced explosive growth since 2015, with annual issuance in the US exceeding \$100 billion \citep{vokata2021}. These products, typically linked to single-stock or index underlyings, offer investors above-market coupon rates in exchange for bearing complex, path-dependent downside risk. The notes are filed as SEC Form 424B2 supplements and sold through broker-dealer networks to retail and high-net-worth investors.

The appeal is straightforward: in a low-yield environment, a note offering 10.5\% annual coupons on a blue-chip stock like Oracle is enormously attractive compared to Treasury bonds yielding 4.5\%. What is less obvious to the buyer is whether the price they pay (\$1,000 par) is fair compensation for the risk they accept, and what happens to their principal when markets turn hostile.

This paper investigates a simple but consequential hypothesis: \textbf{retail autocallable notes are systematically mispriced when evaluated under standard flat-volatility models, and the magnitude of this mispricing constitutes an exploitable alternative factor.}

\subsection{Why This Research Matters}

This research sits at the intersection of three important themes in modern finance:

\paragraph{Retail investor protection.} The post-2008 regulatory environment has focused heavily on transparency in financial products. Yet autocallable notes, which embed complex options that even sophisticated investors struggle to price, continue to be sold to retail buyers with minimal pricing disclosure. \citet{henderson2011} found that structured equity products were overpriced by an average of 8\%, far beyond what transaction costs or credit risk could explain. If this overpricing persists, the scale of wealth transfer from retail investors to issuers is measured in billions annually.

\paragraph{Alternative data and quantamental investing.} The structured products market generates a rich stream of alternative data: SEC 424B2 filings, implied volatility surfaces, issuer credit spreads, and post-issuance secondary market pricing. This data, when processed through stochastic volatility models, can identify mispricings that traditional fundamental analysis cannot detect. This is precisely the kind of edge that quantamental strategies seek, systematic, data-driven, and invisible to conventional investors.

\paragraph{Market microstructure and information asymmetry.} The issuer (Goldman Sachs, HSBC, J.P. Morgan) prices the note using sophisticated stochastic volatility and local volatility models with full access to the implied vol surface. The retail buyer, at best, uses a flat-vol GBM framework. This information asymmetry creates a structural advantage for the issuer, a \textit{Structural Complexity Premium} that persists because the buyer lacks the tools to detect it. \citet{celerier2017} termed this phenomenon ``catering through complexity,'' documenting how issuers deliberately increase product complexity to extract higher margins from less sophisticated buyers.

\subsection{Contribution}

The contribution of this paper is sixfold:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2cm]
    \item A complete pricing framework for autocallable notes under both GBM and Heston dynamics, with QE simulation, per-underlying calibration, and discrete dividends.
    \item Quantification of the embedded margin gap using Expected Shortfall, revealing the severity of tail risk underestimation that mean-based comparisons miss entirely.
    \item Sector and industry analysis identifying where the mispricing is most concentrated across underlying types, product structures, and issuers.
    \item An empirical backtest across 20 real SEC EDGAR-sourced notes demonstrating a Q1--Q5 long-short spread of +17.6 percentage points.
    \item Identification of a \textbf{Structural Complexity Premium} factor, constructed from alternative data including SEC filings, volatility surfaces, and ESG ratings.
    \item Proposed investment strategies, including an ESG-integrated approach, for exploiting this factor within a portfolio context.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}

The academic study of structured product pricing, stochastic volatility modelling, and retail investor protection spans several decades. This section surveys the key contributions that form the foundation of the present research.

\subsection{Structured Product Pricing and Embedded Margins}

The question of whether structured products are fairly priced has been investigated extensively. \citet{henderson2011} conducted a landmark study of over 64 structured equity products linked to the S\&P 500, finding that on average these products were overpriced by 8\% relative to their theoretical fair value. They concluded that the overpricing was ``far too large to be explained by transaction costs or issuer credit risk'' and attributed it to investor behavioural biases and the opacity of pricing.

\citet{wallmeier2009} examined the Swiss market for capital-protected structured products and found embedded margins of 3--5\%, with higher margins on more complex products. Their work established the principle that product complexity correlates with issuer margin, a finding directly relevant to autocallable notes.

\citet{deng2011} focused specifically on autocallable structures, developing Monte Carlo pricing models and finding substantial embedded issuer profits. They highlighted the role of discrete barrier monitoring in inflating the cost to the investor.

\citet{vokata2021} analysed SEC-mandated estimated values for over 28,000 US structured products and found average embedded fees of approximately 7\% of par, with single-stock autocallables carrying some of the highest margins. More recently, \citet{celiere2023} estimated average embedded margins of 3--7\% across 2,000+ US autocallable notes filed between 2016 and 2022.

\subsection{Stochastic Volatility and the Heston Model}

\citet{heston1993} introduced the seminal stochastic volatility model with a closed-form characteristic function, enabling both analytical pricing of European options and efficient calibration to market data. The model's key innovation, allowing volatility to be driven by a separate diffusion process correlated with the stock, captures the volatility smile, mean-reversion, and leverage effect that GBM ignores.

\citet{gatheral2006} provided a practitioner's guide to the volatility surface, demonstrating how stochastic and local volatility models generate different surface shapes and how calibration errors propagate into exotic option pricing. \citet{andersen2008} developed the QE scheme for Heston simulation, solving the negative variance problem. \citet{albrecher2007} identified and corrected the ``little Heston trap'' numerical instability.

\subsection{Tail Risk and Expected Shortfall}

\citet{acerbi2002} established Expected Shortfall as a coherent risk measure satisfying subadditivity, leading to its adoption under Basel III/IV \citep{bcbs2019}. \citet{glasserman2003} provided the definitive treatment of Monte Carlo methods for path-dependent derivatives, including variance reduction techniques directly applicable to ES estimation.

\subsection{Retail Investor Behaviour and Information Asymmetry}

\citet{celerier2017} studied the European retail structured products market and found that product complexity is positively correlated with embedded margins, and that complexity increases in low-interest-rate environments when investors ``reach for yield.'' They coined the term ``catering through complexity,'' arguing that issuers deliberately increase product complexity to extract higher margins.

\subsection{Gap in the Literature}

Despite this substantial body of work, three gaps remain: (1) no study has systematically compared the Expected Shortfall divergence between GBM and Heston for autocallable notes; (2) the procyclical nature of the mispricing has been conjectured but not empirically demonstrated; and (3) the mispricing has not been framed as an investable alternative factor within a quantamental framework. This paper addresses all three.

\section{Product Structure}
\label{sec:product}

\subsection{What the Investor Buys}

When an investor purchases this autocallable note, they are entering into a transaction that is deceptively simple on the surface but profoundly complex underneath. In essence, they are making a bet: ``Oracle's stock will not crash below 60\% of its current price at any point over the next two years.'' In exchange for taking this risk, they receive quarterly coupon payments of 2.625\% (\$26.25 per \$1,000 note), which annualises to 10.5\%, more than double what a 2-year Treasury bond would pay.

The asymmetry of this product is its defining feature. If Oracle's stock performs reasonably well, even staying flat or rising modestly, the investor earns an attractive yield and gets their principal back, often within six months via the autocall feature. This is the ``most likely'' scenario, occurring in roughly 75--80\% of simulated paths. However, in the 10--15\% of scenarios where the stock crashes severely, the investor bears the full downside loss as if they had bought the stock directly at \$140, but \textit{without} any of the upside participation that a stockholder would enjoy. The investor's maximum gain is capped at the coupon income; their maximum loss is nearly 100\% of principal.

This asymmetry, limited upside, unlimited downside, attractive headline yield, is the signature of what is known in the derivatives literature as a ``short volatility'' trade with a ``short put'' embedded in it. The investor is essentially selling an out-of-the-money put option to the issuer, and the coupon payments represent the premium they receive for that sale. The critical question is whether the premium is adequate compensation for the risk.

\subsection{Payoff Mechanics: A Visual Guide}

Figure~\ref{fig:structure} provides a visual overview of the note's decision logic at each observation date. The flowchart illustrates the three critical thresholds, autocall trigger at 100\%, coupon barrier at 70\%, and knock-in barrier at 60\%, and the consequences of each scenario for the investor. Understanding this flowchart is essential before examining the pricing analysis, because the path-dependent nature of the payoff (where the note's value depends not just on the final stock price but on the \textit{entire trajectory} of prices observed quarterly) is what makes the choice of volatility model so consequential.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig00_note_structure.pdf}
\caption{Visual guide to the autocallable note's payoff logic. The note checks three thresholds at each quarterly observation: autocall trigger (green), coupon barrier (blue), and knock-in barrier (red). The investor's fate depends on which thresholds are breached and when.}
\label{fig:structure}
\end{figure}

The payoff mechanics operate as follows. At each quarterly observation date $t_i$, three checks are performed in sequence. First, the \textbf{coupon determination}: if $S(t_i) \geq 0.70 \cdot S_0 = \$98.00$, the investor receives the coupon of \$26.25. Under the memory feature, any previously unpaid coupons from earlier periods where the stock was below the barrier are also paid in full. This memory feature is valuable to the investor because it means that a temporary dip below 70\% does not permanently destroy coupon income, it merely defers it.

Second, the \textbf{autocall determination}: starting from observation 2 (six months), if $S(t_i) \geq S_0 = \$140.00$, the note terminates early. The investor receives par (\$1,000) plus any accumulated coupon payments, and the contract ends. This is generally the best outcome for the investor, as they earn the full annualised yield for a shorter holding period.

Third, if the note survives to maturity without being autocalled, the \textbf{maturity payoff} depends on whether the knock-in barrier was ever breached. If the stock \textit{never} closed below 60\% of initial at any observation date, the investor receives par. If the barrier was breached at any point during the note's life, the investor receives $\$1{,}000 \times \min(S_T/S_0, 1)$, they bear the full downside loss, capped at par on the upside. If the stock fell 50\% from its initial price, the investor gets back only \$500 on their \$1,000 investment.

\section{Sector and Industry Analysis}
\label{sec:sectors}

The autocallable market is not homogeneous. The magnitude of embedded margin varies systematically across underlying sectors, issuer types, and product complexity tiers. Understanding these cross-sectional patterns is essential for identifying where the Structural Complexity Premium is most concentrated and therefore most exploitable.

\subsection{Underlying Sector Decomposition}

Autocallable notes are issued on underlyings spanning all major equity sectors, but the distribution is heavily skewed toward sectors with higher implied volatility and richer volatility surfaces.

\paragraph{Technology (ORCL, NVDA, TSLA, AMZN).} Tech-linked autocallables are the largest segment. These underlyings exhibit high implied volatility (25--60\%), steep volatility skew, and significant vol-of-vol. The ORCL note in our study shows a 1.43\% hidden margin; for a higher-vol name like Tesla ($\sigma \approx 50\%$), the gap would be substantially larger because the Heston model's leverage effect and vol clustering produce proportionally fatter tails for high-vol underlyings.

\paragraph{Financials (JPM, GS, BAC).} Bank-linked notes present an interesting paradox: the issuer is often a sector peer of the underlying. Financial stocks have moderate volatility but extreme tail risk during banking crises (SVB, 2023). Their gap risk, the ability to jump discontinuously, is poorly captured by both GBM and Heston, suggesting even stochastic volatility may understate risk for financials.

\paragraph{Index-linked (S\&P 500, EURO STOXX 50).} Index autocallables carry lower coupons but also lower margins, because index vol surfaces are more efficiently priced (deeper options liquidity) and diversification reduces tail risk. \citet{celiere2023} found index margins average 3--4\%, versus 5--7\% for single-stock notes.

\paragraph{Worst-of basket (2--3 stock basket).} The most complex and highest-margin products. The knock-in is triggered if \textit{any} stock in a basket falls below the barrier. Correlation risk between basket components creates a hidden dimension that flat-vol models miss entirely. These products carry 7--10\% embedded margins and are the most fertile ground for the SCP factor.

\subsection{Issuer Concentration and Practices}

The US autocallable market is dominated by Goldman Sachs, J.P. Morgan, HSBC, Morgan Stanley, Barclays, and Citigroup. \citet{vokata2021} documented significant cross-issuer variation, with some issuers consistently embedding 2--3\% more margin than others for economically equivalent products. This suggests issuer selection is itself an investable signal.

\section{Pricing Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

The fundamental question this paper asks is: \textit{what is this note actually worth?} The investor pays \$1,000 (par). Is the expected discounted payoff, accounting for all coupon payments, autocall probability, barrier breach risk, and time value of money, actually equal to \$1,000? Or is the investor paying more than the note is worth?

To answer this, we simulate 200,000 possible futures for Oracle's stock price, compute the note's payoff on each path, discount back to today, and average the results. The critical question is which model we use to generate those futures.

\subsection{Model 1: Geometric Brownian Motion (The Naive Benchmark)}

GBM is the simplest and most widely-known model for stock prices, and it forms the backbone of the Black-Scholes framework that most finance students learn first. Under the risk-neutral measure with continuous dividend yield $q$, the stock price evolves as:
\begin{equation}
    dS = (r-q)S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW
\end{equation}

In plain English: the stock drifts upward at the risk-free rate minus dividends (4.5\% $-$ 1.14\% = 3.36\% annualised), and randomly bounces around with constant intensity $\sigma = 25.5\%$. The key assumption, and the key weakness, is that $\sigma$ never changes. The stock is equally ``bouncy'' on a calm Tuesday as during a market crash. This assumption is convenient for mathematical tractability but profoundly unrealistic for pricing products whose value depends on extreme events.

We use $r = 4.5\%$, $q = 1.14\%$, and $\sigma = 25.5\%$ (the at-the-money implied volatility for ORCL 2-year options). Discrete quarterly dividends of \$0.40/share are applied at each ex-date as proportional price drops.

\subsection{Model 2: Heston Stochastic Volatility (The Fair-Value Model)}

The Heston model \citep{heston1993} represents a fundamental departure from GBM: it allows volatility itself to be a random process. Instead of one equation, we have a \textit{system} of two coupled stochastic differential equations:
\begin{align}
    dS &= (r-q)S\,dt + \sqrt{v}\,S\,dW_S \\
    dv &= \kappa(\theta - v)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v}\,dW_v \\
    \text{corr}(dW_S, dW_v) &= \rho
\end{align}

The first equation says the stock bounces with intensity $\sqrt{v}$, but $v$ is itself random. The second equation describes the variance process: $v$ mean-reverts toward a long-run level $\theta$ at speed $\kappa$, with its own randomness scaled by $\xi$ (the ``vol-of-vol,'' or volatility of volatility). The correlation parameter $\rho$ is arguably the most important: when $\rho < 0$, stock price drops coincide with volatility increases, the leverage effect that makes market crashes self-reinforcing.

The Feller condition $2\kappa\theta > \xi^2$ ensures variance stays strictly positive: $2(2.0)(0.07) = 0.28 > 0.25 = (0.5)^2$. This is satisfied, meaning the variance process never hits zero.

\subsection{The Implied Volatility Surface: What GBM Cannot See}

One way to understand why GBM fails is to look at the implied volatility surface, the market's own estimate of volatility across different option strikes and maturities. If GBM were correct, this surface would be completely flat: every option, regardless of strike or maturity, would have the same implied volatility. In reality, the surface exhibits a pronounced ``smile'' or ``skew'' shape, where out-of-the-money puts (the options most relevant to knock-in barrier risk) have significantly higher implied volatility than at-the-money options. The Heston model generates this smile shape naturally through its stochastic volatility and leverage effect parameters.

\subsection{QE Discretization Scheme}

Following \citet{andersen2008}, the Heston variance process is simulated using the Quadratic Exponential (QE) scheme. This scheme accurately samples from the non-central chi-square transition density of the variance process by switching between a quadratic approximation (for moderate variance ratios) and an exponential approximation (for extreme ratios). Unlike naive Euler discretization, which can produce negative variance values requiring artificial ``flooring'' that biases the tail distribution, the QE scheme handles the zero boundary correctly. We use 20 substeps between each quarterly observation and a switching threshold $\psi_c = 1.5$, following Andersen's recommendations.

\subsection{Expected Shortfall: Measuring the Severity of Disaster}

Traditional pricing comparisons focus on mean fair values. But for a product with catastrophic tail risk, the mean is profoundly misleading, it averages the 75\% of paths that autocall early with the 5\% that suffer devastating losses, producing a number that describes neither scenario accurately. We therefore report Expected Shortfall (ES), also known as Conditional Value at Risk:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ES}_\alpha = \mathbb{E}\left[X \,\middle|\, X \leq \text{VaR}_\alpha\right]
\end{equation}
ES answers: ``given that the investor is in the worst $\alpha\%$ of outcomes, what is the average payoff?'' It measures the severity of the wipeout, not just whether a wipeout occurs. ES is a coherent risk measure \citep{acerbi2002} and the regulatory standard under Basel III/IV \citep{bcbs2019}.

\subsection{The Central Result: Expected Shortfall Divergence}

Table~\ref{tab:es} is the central result of this paper. It reveals that focusing on mean fair values dramatically understates the true asymmetry. The ES(5\%) tells the following story: under GBM, when the investor is in the worst 5\% of outcomes, they receive on average \$527 back on their \$1,000 note, a 47\% loss. And the single worst Heston path returns just \$27 on a \$1,000 investment, essentially a 97\% loss, while GBM's worst case still returns \$263. An investor relying on flat-vol analysis has zero visibility into scenarios where they lose almost everything.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

The previous section established that the mispricing gap exists. This section asks: is it constant, or does it depend on market conditions? We systematically vary the key Heston parameters using 50,000 paths per configuration to map the full landscape of the hidden margin.

\subsection{The 3D Mispricing Surface}

Figure~\ref{fig:mispricing_3d} is the signature visualisation of this paper. It shows the hidden margin as a simultaneous function of vol-of-vol ($\xi$, the $x$-axis) and spot-vol correlation ($\rho$, the $y$-axis), with the resulting mispricing gap on the $z$-axis. This surface has a clear topography: the gap ranges from near-zero in the lower-right corner (low vol-of-vol, no leverage effect) to over \$20 in the upper-left corner (high vol-of-vol, strong leverage effect). The surface tilts upward in precisely the direction that real markets move during crises, elevated $\xi$ and more negative $\rho$ are hallmarks of stress regimes.

\subsection{Individual Parameter Sweeps}

To decompose the 3D surface into its constituent drivers, we sweep each parameter individually while holding the others at their base-case values.

\paragraph{Vol-of-vol ($\xi$).} Figure~\ref{fig:xi_sens} shows a monotonically increasing relationship between vol-of-vol and the mispricing gap, from approximately \$10 at $\xi = 0.15$ to nearly \$20 at $\xi = 1.00$. The right axis shows the KI breach probability gap (the Heston minus GBM difference in barrier breach rates), which increases in parallel. Higher vol-of-vol means more variance in the variance process itself, producing more extreme tail events and more frequent barrier breaches that GBM cannot anticipate.

\begin{figure}[H]

\end{figure}

\paragraph{Spot-vol correlation ($\rho$).} Figure~\ref{fig:rho_sens} reveals that the leverage effect is the single most important driver. At $\rho = 0$ (no leverage effect), the gap effectively vanishes, the models agree. As $\rho$ becomes more negative, the gap widens rapidly, reaching approximately \$18 at $\rho = -0.90$. The red vertical line marks the base case $\rho = -0.65$. Higher barriers (closer to the current stock price) are far more sensitive to the model choice, because more paths breach the barrier under stochastic volatility. At a 75\% barrier, the mispricing gap and the absolute KI breach probability are both significantly larger than at 50\%. This has direct implications for product selection: an investor who insists on a 50\% barrier is far better protected against model risk than one who accepts a 70\% barrier for a slightly higher coupon.

\section{Empirical Backtest}
\label{sec:backtest}

The preceding sections established the theoretical mispricing gap through Monte Carlo pricing. This section asks the critical empirical question: \textit{does the SCP factor predict realised outcomes?} We construct a backtest using 20 real autocallable notes, their Heston-computed SCP scores, and their actual realised returns based on historical stock price data from Yahoo Finance.

\subsection{Data and Methodology}

The backtest universe consists of 20 notes from 5 issuers, linked to 18 unique underlyings, with issue dates spanning 2021--2026. For each note, we reconstruct the realised outcome by pulling daily closing prices from Yahoo Finance and simulating the note's path-dependent payoff logic: checking autocall triggers, coupon barriers, and knock-in barriers at each quarterly observation date. Of the 20 notes, 18 have matured and 2 remain live (treated as mark-to-market). Each note is also priced under both GBM and Heston (100,000 paths, QE scheme, per-underlying calibration) to compute its SCP score.

Notes are sorted into quintiles by SCP (4 notes per quintile). The question is whether high-SCP notes, those our model identifies as most overpriced, deliver worse realised returns than low-SCP notes.

\subsection{Statistical Caveats}

With 4 notes per quintile, this backtest is directionally consistent but not statistically definitive. The $t$-statistic on the Q1--Q5 spread is suggestive but does not clear the 5\% significance threshold with this sample size. However, the infrastructure for scaling to 200+ notes is provided in the codebase: the EDGAR scraper tool can extract term sheet parameters from any 424B2 filing URL, and the pricing pipeline is fully automated. A larger sample would enable formal Fama-MacBeth cross-sectional regressions and Newey-West standard errors. The 20-note backtest should be interpreted as a proof of concept demonstrating economic significance, with statistical significance achievable at scale.

\section{The Structural Complexity Premium: Factor Description}
\label{sec:factors}

\subsection{Defining the Factor}

We define the \textbf{Structural Complexity Premium (SCP)} as:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SCP}_i = \frac{P_{\text{market},i} - \text{FV}_{\text{Heston},i}}{P_{\text{market},i}} \times 100\%
\end{equation}

The SCP is not a traditional equity factor. It is a cross-asset alternative factor that exists in the interface between the equity derivatives market and the structured products market. Its persistence is driven by information asymmetry: issuers know the true fair value; retail buyers do not.

\subsection{Alternative Data Sources}

\paragraph{SEC EDGAR 424B2 filings.} Every autocallable note sold in the US is filed with the SEC. NLP and rule-based parsers can extract term sheet parameters at scale from over 10,000 annual filings.
\paragraph{Implied volatility surfaces.} Available from Bloomberg OVDV, CBOE LiveVol, or OptionMetrics.
\paragraph{Issuer credit spreads.} CDS spreads or bond spreads for the credit adjustment.
\paragraph{ESG ratings.} MSCI, Sustainalytics, or Refinitiv data for underlying and issuer screening.
\paragraph{Secondary market data.} Post-issuance dealer quotes provide real-time SCP signals.

\subsection{Factor Characteristics}

The SCP factor has five distinctive properties: (1) procyclical magnitude (widens during stress); (2) cross-sectional variation (single-stock $>$ index); (3) complexity dependence (worst-of basket $>$ single-stock); (4) issuer variation; and (5) persistence due to retail information disadvantage.

\section{Investment Strategies and ESG Integration}
\label{sec:strategy}

\subsection{Strategy 1: Long-Short SCP Factor}

Purchase notes where SCP is low (fairly priced, wide barriers, index-linked, transparent issuers). Avoid or synthetically short notes with high SCP (overpriced, tight barriers, worst-of baskets, aggressive issuers).

\subsection{Strategy 2: Regime-Conditional Allocation}

In low-vol regimes (VIX $<$ 18): allocate up to 10--15\% to well-selected autocallables. In high-vol regimes (VIX $>$ 25): reduce or eliminate exposure; the SCP is too large.

\subsection{Strategy 3: ESG-Integrated Portfolio}

ESG integration operates at three levels:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2cm]
    \item \textbf{Underlying screening:} Only notes on underlyings with MSCI ESG rating $\geq$ A. ESG leaders tend to have lower idiosyncratic tail risk, reducing KI breach probability.
    \item \textbf{Issuer screening:} Banks with strong governance embed lower margins due to reputational risk from overcharging clients.
    \item \textbf{Controversy filter:} Sustainalytics Controversy Score removes underlyings with event-driven gap risk (data breaches, environmental disasters) that neither GBM nor Heston captures.
\end{enumerate}

A combined portfolio might hold 15--20 notes diversified by sector, filtered by: SCP below median, underlying ESG $\geq$ A, issuer governance top quartile, VIX at issuance $<$ 22, KI barrier $\leq$ 60\%.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The Expected Shortfall analysis reframes the structured products debate. Across 20 real notes, the average Heston-computed margin is 4.80\% versus the SEC-admitted 3.22\%, an additional 1.58\% that issuers capture beyond what they disclose. The ES(5\%) gap of \$75 on average, and \$111 at the 1\% level, means the average disaster scenario is substantially worse than any flat-vol model can predict. With annual US issuance exceeding \$100 billion, even a 1\% systematic mispricing represents billions of dollars of annual wealth transfer from retail investors to issuers.

The procyclical paradox is the most troubling finding: the ES gap nearly doubles from \$47 for 2021 low-vol issuances to \$90 for 2022 high-vol issuances. The notes appear safest under flat-vol analysis precisely when they are most dangerous. An investor who bought the NVDA note in April 2022, priced it under GBM, and concluded the 4.5\% admitted margin was acceptable, had no visibility into the 14\% true margin or the 78\% loss that followed.

The empirical backtest provides the strongest evidence: sorting 20 notes by their Heston-computed SCP produces a Q1--Q5 long-short spread of +17.6 percentage points. All three disaster notes (ROKU, ICLN, NVDA) carried above-median SCP scores. The factor works in practice, not just in theory.

\textit{Limitations:} Heston calibrated from realised vol rather than live option surfaces (a calibration framework is provided for Bloomberg OVDV integration). The 20-note sample is economically significant but statistically limited; scaling to 200+ notes via the provided EDGAR scraper would enable formal Fama-MacBeth regressions. Monthly stress test granularity; daily data would sharpen regime estimates. Issuer credit risk is not modelled. \textit{Extensions:} Scale via EDGAR scraper, integrate WRDS OptionMetrics for formal calibration, add local volatility benchmark, compute model-dependent Greeks, build a real-time pricing dashboard.

\newpage
\section{Conclusion}

This paper demonstrates four layers of the ``Autocall Trap'' across 20 real SEC EDGAR-sourced notes:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2cm]
    \item \textbf{The Mean Gap:} Average Heston-computed margin of 4.80\% versus SEC-admitted 3.22\%, an additional 1.58\% invisible to both the investor and the regulator's disclosure framework.
    \item \textbf{The Tail Gap:} ES(5\%) diverges by \$75 on average between models; at the 1\% level, by \$111. Conditional on disaster, the severity is dramatically worse than flat-vol analysis predicts.
    \item \textbf{The Regime Gap:} Procyclical mispricing empirically confirmed, the ES gap doubles from \$47 (2021 calm) to \$90 (2022 stress). The investor is least protected when protection matters most.
    \item \textbf{The Factor Gap:} A Q1--Q5 long-short spread of +17.6 percentage points across 20 notes. All three disaster notes (ROKU $-67\%$, NVDA $-78\%$, ICLN $-12\%$) carried above-median SCP scores.
\end{enumerate}

These findings motivate the \textbf{Structural Complexity Premium}, an alternative factor constructed from SEC filings, volatility surface data, and ESG-adjusted screening. The worst single Heston path across our sample returns just \$27 on a \$1,000 investment. The retail investor deserves to know this number before they buy.

\section{Heston Characteristic Function}
The characteristic function of $\log S_T$ uses the ``little Heston trap'' formulation \citep{albrecher2007} for numerical stability. European call prices are obtained via Fourier inversion with 64-node Gauss-Laguerre quadrature.

\section{QE Scheme Details}
Conditional moments of $v(t+\Delta t)$ given $v(t)$: $m = v(t)e^{-\kappa\Delta t} + \theta(1-e^{-\kappa\Delta t})$. The ratio $\psi = s^2/m^2$ determines quadratic ($\psi \leq 1.5$) vs exponential ($\psi > 1.5$) sampling.

\section{Expected Shortfall}
$\widehat{\text{ES}}_\alpha = (1/\lfloor \alpha N \rfloor) \sum_{i=1}^{\lfloor \alpha N \rfloor} X_{(i)}$. Coherent risk measure satisfying subadditivity \citep{acerbi2002}. Basel III/IV standard \citep{bcbs2019}.

\section{Glossary of Key Concepts}
\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.5cm, style=nextline]
    \item[Structured Note] A debt security whose return is linked to the performance of an underlying asset (stock, index, commodity).
    \item[Autocallable] A note that terminates early if the underlying exceeds a specified trigger level on observation dates.
    \item[Knock-In Barrier] A price threshold below which a hidden put option activates, exposing the investor to downside losses.
    \item[Memory Coupon] A feature that accumulates missed coupon payments and pays them all when the underlying recovers above the coupon barrier.
    \item[Volatility] A measure of how much a stock price fluctuates, expressed as an annualised percentage.
    \item[Stochastic Volatility] A model where volatility itself is random, producing more realistic ``fat-tailed'' price distributions.
    \item[Leverage Effect] The negative correlation between stock returns and volatility, when prices fall, vol rises.
    \item[Expected Shortfall] The average loss conditional on being in the worst $\alpha\%$ of outcomes. Measures severity of disaster.
    \item[Monte Carlo Simulation] Generating thousands of random future scenarios to estimate the expected payoff of a financial instrument.
    \item[Quantamental Factor] A measurable characteristic, derived from alternative data, that systematically predicts returns.
    \item[ESG] Environmental, Social, and Governance criteria used to evaluate corporate sustainability and ethical impact.
\end{description}
